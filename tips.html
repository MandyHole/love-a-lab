Top tips

If you decide to buy a Labrador

How to Choose your puppy

The most important thing you need to do is make sure that you choose a reputable breeder. Visit the puppies before making a payment, ensuring that you see the mum with the puppies and that you're satisfied they are living in a healthy environment. All Labrador puppies should be KC registered, vaccinated and microchipped before you bring them home at no younger than 8 weeks old. You should also consider what type of dog you want: working lab/show lab, active/chill, sociable/independent, male/female, submissive/dominant, etc. You can ask the breeder for advice.

What to Buy

Essential Items

  • Crate (and ideally dog mattress)
  • Water/Food Bowls - sturdy or they will get destroyed
  • Collar/Tag -it's the law
  • Poo bags
  • Lead
  • Brush/comb
  • Transportation crate and/or harness/seatbelt

Recommended Items

  • Kong
  • Snuffle Mat
  • Ropes (you'll want a long one!)
  • Ball
  • Long training lead
  • Soft squeaky chew toys
  • Deer antlers are amazing, but only suitable from 4months+

Preparation and Research

Move anything and everything that you don't want to get chewed up as Labs will chew about anything! If you have stairs, put a stair gate up to protect your things and their joints. Dogs shouldn't climb stairs before four months (n.b., Wren hated stairs and wouldn't go up them for chicken, and Daisy will dash up and down them any chance she can get). Make sure you know what is dangerous to your dogs, such as foods and plants (see links below).

General Training Principles

Firstly, find out what motivates your puppy? If it's a Lab, food will definitely be high on the list as well as attention. Use that motivation to reward your puppy for good behaviour and ignore bad behaviour. If you're using food, make sure you take it out of your puppy's food allowance (weigh out the food for the day and then hold some back for training). Use high value food (chicken or a bit of cheese) for recall (a long lead when you're out and about will help ensure its safety). Use really high pitch happy sounds if you're happy with them. Look away and ignore them if they are trying to get your attention with bad behaviour (whining, biting, jumping, etc).

Key Training Area Tips

The most frustrating aspects of young labrador puppies for me are house training and hyperactivity. House training is not fun, but the key to success is consistency and perseverance! Take the puppy out every 30minutes or so: after meals, when they first wake up, if they've been up for awhile, etc. Give a treat and lots of praise when they do their business where they should (relating it to a keyword for training helps too). Ignore mistakes unless you catch them in the act in which case you should scoop them up, take them out and give the training word you decide on. If they start going a bit crazy, get their nose to the ground, which gives them mental stimulation, helps harness their sense of smell and calms them down.
